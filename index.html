Save Zavitsa & Mount Parnon

This is not about the view. It is about the water supply for 50,000 acres of farmland — and the communities that depend on it.

Why This Matters

Mount Zavitsa is karst limestone — a geological sponge. Rain disappears into the rock, filtering through underground fractures and conduits toward the coast. The Parnon karst system has been documented by dye-tracing experiments to carry water from inland sinkholes to the Anavalos submarine springs on the Argolic Gulf, a journey of 8 days underground, documented since Pausanias in the 2nd century AD. Those springs irrigate the entire Thyrean and Argive agricultural plains.

Blasting and concreting the ridge to install wind turbine foundations shatters those underground conduits. If freshwater pressure drops in a coastal karst aquifer, seawater fills the void — salinising agricultural wells and ending cultivation that has continued since antiquity. This is not an aesthetic objection. It is a water security alarm.

The permits were obtained by design across two municipalities — North Kynouria for Zavitsa, South Kynouria for Parnon/Kosmas — to divide local resistance and avoid a unified environmental review of a single ecosystem. On May 25, 2026, the North Kynouria municipal council, joined by representatives of twelve communities, voted against the project. Today's march on Zavitsa is that vote made in person.

Two deadlines are live right now. Greece's public consultation on a new renewable energy spatial framework closes June 24. For the first time the framework prohibits wind farms above 1,200 metres — but licensed projects are exempt under EU fast-track rules. Public submissions during the consultation period create a permanent legal record. And Greece must file a full UNESCO Management Plan for the 516,412-hectare Parnon Biosphere Reserve by September 30, 2027 — once codified, it makes future industrial permitting across the mountain far harder to obtain.
